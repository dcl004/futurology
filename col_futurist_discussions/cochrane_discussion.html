Peter Cochrane's Discussion (Keynote Speech)

Below is Peter Cochrane's keynote presentation at the 18th International Conference on Enginnering Design, 15-18 August 2011, titled "When Machines Design Machines!", in which, he explains how human inventions have advanced beyond human understanding, and thus, require assistance from artificial intelligence to help design and understand them.

Commentary

Until recently, we were the sole designers of technology, alone in the driving seat of making all of the decisions; however, our inventions have exceeded in complexity, what human ability to understand, govern, and control is capable of. Machines are now capable of doing a wide variety of tasks with much greater efficiency than humans including performing more trades than humans on the stock market, controlling our power, water, gas and food supplies, managing our elevators, micro-climates, automobiles and transport systems, and manufacturing nearly everything. Thus, it is not surprising that even now, machines are now heavily involved in the design and creation of more complex systems, as other machines. From the chips that power our computers and mobile devices to robots and commercial processing plants on which we depend heavily on, all have been largely designed by machines.

So the controversy arrises: Will we be completely usurped in our purpose, or are we looking at a new symbiosis with human and artificial intelligences combined to realize the best outcomes possible?

In most of these respects, we don't have much of a choice. It is because of human inability to understand the growing complexity of current and evolving systems that we must now rely on the help of machine intelligence to solve problems for us. To be able to achieve the best results, we now have to use computer modeling, visualization and decision supports. These are also our means to new materials, processing, production and thinking that enables us to revitalize our industries and realize sustainable solutions. Our increasing reliance of machine intelligence may be another industrial, technological revolution.

Thus, far from being completely obsoleted in the design loop of new technologies and maintenance of systems on which we are dependent on, we find ourselves with a new and more vital role in this new revolution. To oversee, engineer, and be consumers/dependents of these machine intelligence.
