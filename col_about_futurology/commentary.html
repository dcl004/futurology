About this Site (Futurology)

Futurology is the projection of the future based on past and recent trends. It can cover a wide variety of fields from politics, to history, to science. In other words, futurology is defined as "the study of the future". In the narrowed focus of the sciences and technologies, futurology, as depicted by popular media and fiction will likely leave many people either disappointed or fearful of what's to come. For instance, Hollywood has done a good job of scaring the pants off the general population by showcasing future technologies in a more apocalyptic light through machine intelligence such as Hal9000 from the Space Odyssey and robots from the Terminator. But actual trends of technological development have shown to be very different from this.

Futurists such as Rachel Armstrong and Brian Johnson claim that technologies, when conceived, are designed to satisfy problems of efficiency and/or impossibility. This is in large part because humans have progressed by thinking and expecting in a linear fashion. We are accustomed to a linear, well-behaved world in which we usually deal with no more than seven variables at any one time. But as interactions in the world become faster and complex ecosystems such as national banking systems grow far too complex beyond human understanding, we find our reliance and symbiosis with technology to be more apparent. Regarding technologies with an efficiency-oriented goal, issues of efficiency often result from an excess of time and resource inputs, high learning curves, or effort needed to satisfy complex tasks. For instance, a current technology that is constantly evolving today, 3D printing, aims to produce efficiency in manufacturing by reducing the above criteria. Its usage today is highly versatile to the manufacturing of parts and goods, making even the construction of large things such as housing complexes cheaper in resource and worker resources by reducing waste and thus, general inputs. Although the positive upsides are easily apparent, the repercussions in economy have dependencies on the supply and demand for these resources.

Similarly, as a result of the growing complexity of current systems, it has become impossible to understand, and thus work with, such systems without much assistance from robot intelligence. Futurist Peter Cochrane has shown artificial intelligence, or AI, are now involved in the design of things like oil rigs, refineries, and the wiring systems of electronics and aircrafts that have become much too complex for humans to comprehend. Even in creativity, in a survey conducted by Scientific American in 2001, AI have recreated old, patented inventions that eclipsed the performances of its older counterparts. Even in new technologies, such as planetary rovers and futuristic antennas, the designs go beyond humans' current capabilities for design, thus creating our fear of being usurped in creative capacities.

The purpose of this site is to provide a deeper understanding of the implications of current and up-and-coming technologies. By showcasing potential developments of future sciences and technologies in various fields, the website hopes to provide an understanding of how technologies are to grow and to invoke thoughts on what these technologies affect in the environments (possibly encompassing social and economic factors) in which they are introduced.
