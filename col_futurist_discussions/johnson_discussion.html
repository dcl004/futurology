Brian Johnson's Discussion (Keynote Speech)

Below is Brian Johnson's keynote presentation at Compute Midwest. In his presentation titled "The Future of Technology", Brian Johnson goes into what futurology is, the different subfields that exist in this study, and what it is futurists do.

Commentary

To start off his presentation, Johnson introduces himself as both a principle engineer and futurist. This means that his job entails both projecting and modeling what what it would be like living as a human with technology 10 to 15 years into the future, and to model the specs that new chipsets would need to measure up to. He goes on to saying that to develop future technologies, these technologies first need to be conceptualized, as "nothing great was ever built by human beings that was not imagined first". But Johnson sees a problem with this depiction. He asks questions such as "Where are the baby toys? Where are the pillows? Where are the family photos?" He asks these to raise a point on human diversity in beliefs and cultures, to show that depictions of future technologies devoids humans of what makes them intrinsically human.

This, he says, is a more accurate projection of what humans using future technology is going to look like. Comparing the two depictions, Johnson says that the second, more accurate depiction is more comfortable and that is ultimately the goal of technology, "to fit beautifully into people's lives".

Johnson's projection of the future of technology falls very much in line with what other futurists believe. That is, he believes that technologies, when conceived, are designed to satisfy some niche that is efficiency and convenience. This is realized by understanding in which area (economy, finance, medicine, etc.) this technology is to be used for, to make more convenient and resource efficient a pre-existing technology in that area, and to make it simpler.

There is a key moment in his speech where Johnson talks about big data technologies, which are technologies in which very large amounts of data are exchanged and modified using complex algorithms talking to other systems with their own complex algorithms. At this moment, Johnson essentially sums up the overarching argument his speech is attempting to make by reminding the audience that technologies can do a lot of things such as making us "more healthy, happier, more productive, and more efficient, all that stuff, [but] in this age of big data and in this secret life of data, you always have to remember that all of this data all of intelligence is meaningless until it comes back and touches the lives of people".
