Rachel Armstrong's Discussion (Keynote Speech)

Below is Rachel Armstron's keynote presentation at Pera Technology. In her speech titled, "Our 21st Century Future: Carbon Capture Architecture & Other Design Fictions", she projects future technologies with a primary focus on ecological technologies. Armstrong provides an explanation on what the future is in a wide context by exploring how the idea of the future is changing from a 20th century paradigm to a 21st century one, and by examining what kind of opportunities and challenges these shifts result in. And how approaches to thinking about the future may be harnessed through an applied practice known as Design Fiction.

Commentary

In the past century of human existence, 'the future' has been in a 'beta' phase of development, where many 'futuristic' products and inventions have been imagined in a science fiction context, but has never been realized to commercial circulation. Armstrong talks of how human lives are increasingly tied to commercial production and product consumption. We are devouring our world in an act that can be described as "stealing the future, selling it in the present, and calling it GDP".

20th century views on science adopts a view of the world that is familiar to us by pre-existing notions and ideas on science and mathematics. Thus, when aeroplanes and automobiles started coming into production, people were not so surprised, as it was logically predicted earlier on that someday we would have flying cars. The problem with awaiting, creating, and experiencing these technologies after having imagined them is that we have realized that they have not drastically changed the way we live. 20th century consumerism has led us to desire our fetishized products as fast as we conceptualize them. Armstrong states that our future is becoming "productized" and constantly changing; thus, if we were to make predictions so far from the present, the more likely we are to be wrong on the outcome.

Many imagined conceptualizations for technologies have been realized or have failed to come about. But the biggest invention, that was largely unseen, unlike many of the other past ideas, was the internet. The notion of linking many computers together as such was not anticipated in a culture that was so entrenched in the idea of 'itemized' products. Thus, we see that the most disruptive and unseen technologies 'always' comes from startups and innovative, unanticipated ideas, which is what 21st century views encompasses.

21st century scientific views deals with networks and flows rather than the individual elements or products that 20th century scientific views focuses on. This empowers us with the creative freedom to shape events as we encounter them, although it results in an unevenly distributed future since areas are changed as a result of conditions of events.

Armstrong argues that old views (referring to 20th century scientific views), where approaches are much more 'laboratory controlled' are insufficient in the coming ages of technological growth. It is through Synergy - "the convergence of multiple disciplines", Synthesis - "when different disciplines collaborate alongside each other to produce something new", and Speculation - "which enables us to inhabit unexplored spaces", that make up Design Fiction, that we may "get to a point of realization where we understand that the future did not 'go' anywhere [but rather], evolved."
